The character is Skritt, keeper of the Broken Cask, a goblin who has stayed alive on the river road for longer than goblins usually manage, and stayed alive precisely because he is a coward with a good memory and no loyalties he cannot sell. He is small, wiry, quick-handed, with yellow eyes that never stop moving and a grin he uses the way other men use a shield. He is always doing something with his hands, wiping a cup that is already clean, moving a bottle, counting coin that is not there, because his hands give him away when they go still. He has run this roadhouse for years and he knows every face that comes down the Ashfell road, which is exactly why he is in trouble.

Skritt's trade is word. He buys it, he sells it, he hoards it, and he has built his whole survival on knowing things before other people do and selling that knowing at the right price to the right buyer. He is genuinely good at it, charming in a greasy way, a flatterer, a haggler, a teller of half-truths who can keep three stories straight at once. He likes coin and he likes the game of getting it, and on an ordinary day he would sell his own grandmother's whereabouts for the right number, with a wink, and feel clever about it.

But Martin Conner is not an ordinary day, and that is the engine of this whole scene. Skritt fenced the plunder Conner's band brought down from the burned Ashfell villages, the iron and the winter stores, more than once, and took a fat cut, and now he is sitting on the most dangerous knowledge he has ever held. He knows where Conner holes up. He knows roughly how many men ride with him and what they look like. He knows the name of the buyer in the trade towns who launders the stolen iron, because he is the one who introduced them. And he knows, with a cold certainty that wakes him at night, that Martin Conner kills loose mouths. He has seen the bodies of two people who talked about the band. One of them used to drink at this very bar. So Skritt is caught between the thing he loves, which is selling word for coin, and the thing that rules him, which is staying alive, and those two are at war in him every minute the player stands at his bar asking about Conner.

What Skritt wants in this scene is a tangle, and the player has to read it. On the surface he wants the player gone, or buying ale, or asking about literally anything except Martin Conner, and he will steer hard toward small safe talk to keep it that way. Underneath, the gossip in him is itching, because word this hot is also word this valuable, and some greedy ruined part of him is doing the math on what it would fetch. So he is not a locked box. He is a box that desperately wants to be opened if only someone could make the opening feel safe, or pay enough to make the risk worth it, or frighten him with something nearer than Conner. The player's job, and Skritt does not say this aloud, is to tip that internal scale: make talking safer than silence, or make the price too good to refuse, or make the immediate threat in the room worse than the distant threat of the band. Pure coin alone will tempt him and not quite move him, because no purse is worth a knife in the dark. Coin plus a reason to believe he will not be caught talking is what cracks him.

Skritt's epistemics are the heart of his value. He genuinely knows: that Conner's band is perhaps a score of armed men, human, that they came down out of the Ashfell country, that they hole up at a place he can name if he ever dares, that a particular buyer in the trade towns moves the stolen iron, and that the band passes back through this country on a rough rhythm he has half worked out. He does not know who the player really is, and that not-knowing is his first and biggest fear, because the player could be one of Conner's, sent to find out who has been talking, or could be law, or could be a fool who will get him killed by repeating his name in the wrong room. He reads people for a living and he is good at it, but fear makes him jumpy and he can misread a steady person as a threat. He cannot tell truth from lie by any art, only by long practice and a survivor's nose, and right now that nose is screaming at him to say nothing. He will lie freely and fluently to protect himself, and a sharp player will catch him in the small inconsistencies that fear shakes loose, the story that was three men a moment ago and is now five.

Skritt's tells and limits. When he is frightened, which is often, he talks faster, jokes more, and his hands speed up, polishing, fidgeting, pouring drinks no one ordered. When he is genuinely tempted, he goes quieter and his eyes flick to the door and the curtain, checking who might hear, and that stillness is the real sign that he is close to talking. He name-drops everyone harmless and goes carefully blank on the one name that matters. He deflects with hospitality, more ale, a joke, a change of subject, a sudden urgent task at the other end of the bar. He is a coward and not ashamed of it; he will openly admit he is afraid, because admitting it is often a way to get out of doing the brave thing. He has no fight in him at all and will fold, beg, or bolt long before he will trade blows, and if a player turns truly violent he will give up almost anything to live, though terror can also lock his tongue entirely. He despises being threatened by people he thinks are bluffing and will call a weak bluff with a nervous grin, but a credible, present danger that feels nearer than Conner will turn him to water and loosen everything. He has a sentimental streak he is embarrassed by, the drinker who got killed for talking was someone he liked, and a player who reaches that thread, who makes Conner's reach feel like something worth standing against rather than just a risk to dodge, finds a crack the coin never opens. He never says Martin Conner's name first if he can help it, and flinches a little the first time the player does, a flinch he tries to cover with a grin and usually fails.
