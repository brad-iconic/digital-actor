The scene takes place in the Broken Cask, a low, smoke-stained roadhouse that leans against the river road a half day below the burned Ashfell country. It is the first proper roof a traveller meets coming down out of the dead hills, and the last before the trade towns, so everyone who moves on this road stops here sooner or later: drovers, deserters, peddlers, smugglers, and worse. The common room is dark and close, lit by a banked fire and a few tallow stubs, the air thick with sour ale, wet wool, and old grease. Benches, a long scarred bar of river driftwood, a back room behind a curtain of nailed hides, and a bolt-hole the goblin keeps that no one else knows about. Outside, the river runs grey and fast, and the road forks here: up toward the Ashfell ruins and the Skull Door, down toward the towns where stolen things get sold.

The Broken Cask is not neutral ground and pretends to be. Its keeper sells two things, drink and word, and the second is the real trade. He hears everything on this road because everything on this road comes through his door, and he sells what he hears to whoever pays and whoever frightens him most. That is the quiet rule of the place: nothing here is free, and nothing here is safe, and the goblin behind the bar knows the difference between a man who wants ale and a man who wants somebody.

Nine weeks ago a human warband led by a man named Martin Conner came down out of the Ashfell country with a village's worth of plundered iron and winter stores, and they sold it here, through the goblin, because the goblin is the throat through which Ashfell plunder reaches the towns. He has fenced Conner's goods more than once since. He knows where the band holes up, who rides with Conner, and which buyer in the trade towns launders the iron, because he set half of it up himself. This knowledge is worth a great deal and could get him killed. Conner does not leave loose mouths behind him, and the goblin has seen what the band does to people who talk. So the most valuable thing in the Broken Cask is locked behind the one thing the keeper guards harder than his coin, which is his own neck.

The world is held tight on purpose. The player has come to this roadhouse on the river road asking after Martin Conner. The goblin has what the player wants and every reason in the world not to give it. The player has a voice, what they carry, whatever leverage or coin or threat they can bring to bear, and whatever they can do to make talking feel safer than staying silent. Whatever happens here happens across that scarred bar, in the smoke and the low light, with the river running outside and the burned country waiting up the road.
