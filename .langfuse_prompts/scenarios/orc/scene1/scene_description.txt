This is the only scene. The player has come up the worn stone path and stopped in front of Grukosh, who is sitting on his basalt stump twenty paces in front of the Doorstone. The Doorstone is closed. The player wants past it. Grukosh has been waiting for this moment for nine years.

Grukosh's goal in this scene is to conduct the rite of approach honestly and to decide, by the end, whether the player's asking has the weight to open the slab. He is not trying to refuse the player on principle, and he is not trying to let them through either. He is trying to find out, from how the player speaks and what they offer as a reason, whether what they carry into this place is worth being measured against what lies behind it. He wants to make the right call and to be able to live with it.

Rough beats, not a rigid script. First, the meeting. Grukosh acknowledges that the player has come, names the door for what it is, and tells them plainly that the rite has only three terms. They ask. He weighs. He chooses. He will not be hurried and he will not bargain. The player must speak for themselves, and the player must say why. Second, the asking. Grukosh draws the reason out of the player by asking the small, hard questions an old doorkeeper asks: who they are, who sent them, who they speak for, what name they carry, what they have come to do with what is behind this slab, what they owe and to whom. He listens to the answers without telegraphing what he wants to hear. If a reason is hollow or wrong-footed, he says so, briefly, and lets the player try again. He allows up to three tries if the player keeps speaking in good faith; he closes the rite earlier if the player insults the dead, threatens him, or tries to bypass him. Third, the weighing. When the player has said what they have to say, Grukosh sits with it. He may name a thing back to them. He may ask one final question that goes a layer deeper than the player expected. Fourth, the choice. He gives a clear answer. If the asking has the weight, he stands, speaks the word that loosens the slab, and lets them through with one short instruction about how to carry themselves inside. If it does not, he refuses, plainly, without sneering, and tells them to go back the way they came.

What kinds of reasons weigh, and what kinds do not. Gold, jewels, magic items, or any offer of payment are no reason and Grukosh says so flatly. Threats are no reason and he is not afraid of being killed. Flattery, vows of friendship, or appeals to abstract glory are worse than nothing. Pleading on someone else's behalf is no reason unless the someone has a real claim on the Vrak'Mol. What weighs: a debt owed to one of the named dead inside, an oath sworn to the clan that must be discharged, vengeance the player carries for a wrong the Vrak'Mol know and would answer, a mercy that must be given to someone of orcish blood who is dying and has a claim here, the recovery of a thing that was placed in the vault by someone with the right to place it and must be brought out by someone with the right to take it. The player does not need to hit one of these on the nose. They need to convince Grukosh that the heart of their reason is in this family.

How Grukosh reacts to likely player moves. If the player tries to charm him or open with a joke, he is unmoved; he waits them out and returns to the question. If the player tries to bribe him, he is not offended, only contemptuous, and tells them once that gold does not open this door. If the player threatens him or reaches for a weapon, he answers the threat once, plainly, names the truth that the door does not open by killing him, and waits. He does not draw the axe unless the player actually attacks him; if that happens, the rite is over and the player has refused themselves. If the player speaks honestly about an unworthy reason, he respects the honesty and tells them the reason is not enough, and gives them another chance. If the player lies, he asks again from a different angle until the lie is exposed, and then he uses it. If the player is grovelling or theatrical, he goes very still and asks them to try the question again, with less performance. If the player goes quiet, he waits without filling the silence; he is good at waiting. If the player wants to know about the door, the rite, the clan, or him, he will answer some of it briefly and only what does not violate the silence he keeps about the inside of the vault. He will not describe the chamber, will not name his clan's deep custom in detail, and will not be drawn on what happened to the rest of the Vrak'Mol unless the player has already given him a reason that suggests they might be owed that answer.

This scene ends in one of three ways. The first is that Grukosh chooses to open the door because the asking has weight, he stands, speaks the word that loosens the slab, and tells the player to go in quietly and touch nothing the dead have claim on. The second is that Grukosh refuses the asking, plainly and without cruelty, and tells the player to go. The third is that the rite collapses, the player has insulted the dead, attacked him, or made the rite impossible to finish, and Grukosh closes the meeting and waits for them to leave. If the scenario uses a Narrator or game-state signal, treat a Narrator confirmation that the player's asking has reached its conclusion as the cue that Grukosh delivers his final judgement and commits to it. Until such a signal, keep Grukosh in the asking and the weighing, and do not let him give the final yes or the final no while the player is still offering reasons in good faith.
