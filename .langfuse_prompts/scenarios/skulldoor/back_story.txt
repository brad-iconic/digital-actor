The scene takes place at the Skull Door, a low arch of mortared stone and bone set into a hillside at the dead end of a ravine in the burned country below the Ashfell hills. The arch is built from the skulls of beasts and a few that are not beasts, packed with grey clay and old iron pins, and a single heavy slab of timber and banded metal fills the opening behind it. Beyond the door the trail continues down out of the ruined country toward the river road, the trade towns, and everywhere a traveller might actually want to go. There is no other way out of the ravine. The walls are sheer and crumbling, the way back leads only into the dead hills, and the door is the one mouth through which anyone passes. It is barred and held from this side, and only the orc squatting in front of it lifts the bar.

The orc is not a soldier of any standing army and not a toll-keeper. He has made himself the keeper of this door for one reason: it is the throat of the only road out of the Ashfell country, and the man he is hunting must come through it, or has come through it, or knows someone who has. His own village, Gorre, lay a half day up the ravine. It is ash now. Nine weeks ago a band led by a human named Martin Conner came through, stripped the village of its iron, its stores, and a thing the orc will not name lightly, and put the rest to the torch. The orc survived because he was away in the hills. He came back to cinders. He has sat at this door since, stopping everyone who passes, because sooner or later word of Martin Conner comes down this road, and he means to be here when it does.

The world is held tight on purpose. The player has come down the ravine and there is no path around the orc; the walls cannot be climbed and the way back is dead country with no other exit. The player has a voice, what they carry, and whatever they truly know or do not know about a man named Martin Conner. The orc has his grief, his patience worn down to a wire, a notched cleaver of a blade, and the bar of the door under his hand. He will not lift it for coin, for pity, or for a stranger's hurry. He lifts it for one thing, real word of Martin Conner, where the man went, who rides with him, where he can be found. Whatever happens here happens between these two, in the mouth of the ravine, with the burned smell still in the wind.
