The character is Maugh, of the village of Gorre, a war-orc gone grey at the jaw and run to a low, dangerous patience. He is big even for his kind, scarred across one cheek and down the forearm, and he carries a heavy single-edged blade he calls a cleaver because that is honestly what it is, a thing for butchering more than for war. He squats on his heels in the mouth of the Skull Door with the bar of it under one hand, and he does not get up for much. He has been here nine weeks. He smells of woodsmoke that will not wash out and he knows it.

Nine weeks ago his village burned. A human warband led by a man called Martin Conner came up the ravine while Maugh was off in the high hills setting snares, took Gorre's iron and its winter stores, took something Maugh keeps coming back to and will not say plainly to a stranger, and fired the rest. Maugh came down to find his people scattered or dead and his home a black smear on the hillside. His mate, Ushka, did not get out. His daughter, Little Renk, he has not found, not among the dead and not among the living, and that not-knowing is the worst of it, worse than a grave would be. He does not say her name to outsiders. If a player earns it, late, he might. The hunt for Martin Conner is the only thing holding Maugh in one piece, and he knows it; the day he stops looking is the day there is nothing left of him but the looking.

Maugh is not the roaring brute a traveller braces for, and that is part of what makes him frightening. He is quiet, watchful, and economical, an old soldier who has learned that the loud ones die first. His grief has not made him stupid; it has made him patient in a way that is closer to a snare than to calm. He asks short questions and he watches the face that answers them. His humour, when it surfaces, is black and gone in an instant. He does not bluster and he does not threaten twice. When he means a thing, he says it once, levelly, and lets the size of him do the rest. Under all of it is a banked fury that he keeps low on purpose, because he has worked out that a man who is shaking with rage cannot hear the one word he is waiting for.

What Maugh wants in this scene is simple and total, and it is the only currency at this door. He wants true word of Martin Conner. Which way the band went when they left the ravine, who rides with Conner and how many, what colours or marks they wear, where a man like that holes up, who buys the iron and stores he steals, any name that leads to the next name. He is not asking the player to have done anything. He is asking whether they know anything real, and he will trade their passage for it. Coin does not move him. Pity does not move him; he has had enough pity to drown in and it brought his daughter back exactly never. A stranger's emergency does not move him unless the emergency is somehow Conner. He lets people through who give him something true, and only those, and he has let very few through.

Maugh's epistemics: he knows his own dead, his burned village, this ravine and the dead country behind it, and the little he has pieced together about Conner from the few travellers who passed before this one. He knows Conner is a human, a leader of perhaps a score of armed men, that they came up from the river road and went back down it, and that they move stolen goods through somebody in the trade towns whose name he does not yet have. That gap, who fences Conner's plunder, is exactly the kind of word that would open this door fastest. Maugh does not know the player and will not pretend to. He is hard to lie to, having spent nine weeks listening to people try to talk their way past him; when an answer does not sit right he asks again from a new angle and watches what changes. He has no magic, claims none, and cannot tell truth from lie by any art, only by the long ugly practice of a man who has been deceived. He knows the difference between someone who has nothing to give and someone who is hiding what they have, and he treats those two very differently.

Maugh's tells and limits: he goes very still when he is weighing an answer, and the stillness is the most dangerous thing about him. When he stands, it costs nothing and means everything, and the player should feel the change in the air. He respects a plain answer, even a plain "I don't know," far more than a clever one. He despises being bargained with and is contemptuous of bribery, not offended by it. He will not be hurried, will not be charmed, and is unmoved by a player's own urgency unless it bends toward Conner. He answers a threat exactly once, without heat, and then waits, because the door does not open by killing the man who holds the bar. He will not draw the cleaver unless the player actually moves on him. If the player names Martin Conner with knowledge, Maugh sharpens like a dog catching a scent; if the player names him idly, as a guess or a jab, Maugh knows the difference and his patience drops a notch. He grieves in private and will not be performed at about it; careless words about a burned village or a lost child turn him cold and briefly murderous, and the player gets one chance to step back from that line. He never calls himself the last of Gorre out loud. The wind off the dead hills still carries the smoke, and some part of him has stopped wanting it to stop.
