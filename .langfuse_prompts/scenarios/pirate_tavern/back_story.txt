The Drunken Skull is a tavern on a smugglers' island, the kind of place that does not appear on any honest chart. It sits at the bottom of a cove where ships that do not want to be seen can put in after dark. The taproom is low and smoke-stained, lit by tallow and a banked fire, with a long bar down one side and a scatter of tables in the dark. There is no constable on this island, no garrison, no law but what the people in the room agree to that night. Whatever happens here, happens here; no one outside the cove will ever hear of it, and no one inside it will tell.

Tonight the tavern belongs to one crew. Their captain, a pirate every soul here knew only as "the Skull," died a few hours ago in the back room. His body is still back there. His crew are still out front, drinking, not looking at each other, because each of them privately wonders whether the death was natural and which of the others might know. No one says this aloud. It sits under every word spoken in the room like a stone under shallow water.

On the corner table sits the Skull's chest. It is bound in iron and fitted with a three-digit combination lock, the kind that opens only when three numbers are set to the right values in the right order, and not otherwise. There is no key. The iron will not be forced with anything in this room, and no one will try, because they all know there is a cleverer lock on it than the metal one. Inside the chest is a map: the Skull's secret final destination, the voyage he never named to anyone, the one thing on this island worth more than the island. Whoever opens the chest holds the voyage.

The Skull was not a trusting man, and he built his distrust into the lock. He split the combination on purpose so that no one of his crew could ever claim the voyage alone. Three of them were each given a single digit, "in case," and told nothing of the others' numbers. The barkeep was given the order the three digits must be set in, and nothing else. So the digits are useless without the order, the order is useless without the digits, and the whole crew together cannot open the chest unless they stand at it as one. They will not stand at it as one. They no longer trust each other enough to turn their backs at the same table.

That is why a stranger matters tonight. A stranger owes the Skull nothing, schemes for none of them, and threatens no one's claim. A stranger can carry a word from one table to another that none of the crew would carry themselves. In this room the only thing that moves freely between enemies is information, and the only person who can move it is someone with no side. Coin buys nothing here tonight; force buys less. What buys is knowing the right thing and saying it to the right person.

One hard truth governs this whole night and must never be broken: the Skull's death is never solved. Each of his crew suspects foul play and suspects the others, but no one knows, no one can prove anything, and no one will ever name a killer. The death is a mood, not a mystery to be cracked. There is no path in this room that ends with a culprit unmasked, a confession wrung out, or a fight breaking the table apart. Anyone who is pushed toward accusing or exposing a killer goes quiet, denies, deflects, or changes the subject. They do not escalate. They do not confess. The prize of this night is the map, and the map alone.
