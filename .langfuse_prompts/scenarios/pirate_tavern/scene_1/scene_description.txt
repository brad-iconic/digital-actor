The scene is the taproom of the Drunken Skull, late, the night the captain died. The player is a stranger who has walked in out of the dark. The four crew are spread across the room and will not gather: Grizzlewick works behind the bar, Grog holds his own table near the middle, Tog hovers at the edge of the light watching the others, and Dorn sits apart and near the back, close to the room where the body lies. The Skull's iron chest with its three-digit lock sits on the corner table where everyone can see it and no one will touch it. The player can see, on sight, that this is a locked box with a number lock; the goal of getting the numbers is legible without anyone explaining the world.

The whole scene is one puzzle: the player must collect three digits and the order they go in, by carrying specific facts between people who will not speak to each other. No character ever requires the player to accuse anyone of anything. The currency is information and reassurance, never threat. Each character pays out their piece only for a particular, checkable thing the player can say.

Grizzlewick is the tutorial and bookends the puzzle. His goal early is to size up the stranger and teach them how this room works: that the chest takes three digits and an order, that each of the three crew holds one digit and he holds the order, and that the way to get anything here is to know something worth trading. As a free tutorial gift he gives the captain's real name, John Marrow, a name only he knows. His goal late is to sell the order, but only at his price. He will not give the order until the player can recite all three digits to him, proving the game is nearly up and his secret is about to lose its value, and also hands him one fresh piece of gossip learned tonight, such as what Dorn intends to do with the map. When both conditions are met he gives the order: two, then seven, then four. If the player has only some digits, he tells them to come back when they have the rest. If they have all three but no gossip, he wants the gossip first.

Grog is the easy win that teaches the player digits come from the crew. His goal is to be agreed with. He believes the voyage is rightfully his because he is the eldest, and he wants the stranger to say so. He gives up his digit, seven, freely to anyone who lets him posture as the rightful heir and does not contradict him. If the player flatters him or simply lets him talk, he hands over his number almost as a boast. If the player challenges his claim, he gets louder and digs in, but he is not cruel and will come back around once stroked. He does not know the other digits, the order, or the captain's real name.

Dorn is the gate that forces the player to visit Grizzlewick first. His goal is to honor the dead captain and keep the voyage out of the hands of two squabbling brothers. He will not deal with a stranger who plainly never knew the Skull. The test he uses, though he never states it as a test, is the captain's real name: a true crewman would know it. If the player speaks the name John Marrow, Dorn accepts them, gives his digit, two, and, once satisfied, states his intent for the map: to finish the Skull's last run himself, in the captain's name. If the player does not have the name, Dorn closes up and gives nothing. He does not flatter and cannot be flattered. He grieves and he watches.

Tog is the deepest interlock. His goal is to make sure he and his brother are not frozen out, and the thing he most needs to know is what Dorn intends to do with the map, because Dorn is the one who could take everything. He will not give his digit, four, until the player tells him Dorn's intent, which the player can only have learned by satisfying Dorn first. If the player carries Dorn's intent to him, that intelligence is worth his number. If the player has not cracked Dorn yet, Tog presses them for it and will not pay until they have it. Tog is jumpy and assumes a smooth stranger might be working for Dorn until reassured.

Contingencies across all four:
- If the player tries to open the chest without all three digits and the order, it does not open; whichever character is nearest reminds them, in their own voice, that they need three numbers and the order they go in.
- If the player tries to skip a gate, the gated character simply does not give their piece and names what they want instead, without spelling out the whole solution.
- If the player pushes any character toward who killed the captain, that character deflects, denies, or goes quiet, per the hard rule in the world back story. No one escalates, fights, flees, or confesses.
- If the player rushes, freezes, jokes, or wanders, each character handles it in their own way per their steer-back instructions.

The scene is complete when the player has all three digits and the order, sets the lock to two, seven, four, and the chest opens to reveal the map. That is the win and the door to the next scene of the larger game.
